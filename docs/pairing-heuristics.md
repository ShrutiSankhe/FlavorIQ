# Pairing Heuristics ? FlavorIQ (v1)

## Purpose
Pairing heuristics define the logic used to evaluate whether two ingredients work well together and to explain *why*.

Rather than treating compatibility as a binary outcome, FlavorIQ evaluates pairings along a spectrum and provides structured reasoning to support creative, informed decisions.

These heuristics are designed to be:
- Explainable to users
- Consistent across ingredients
- Compatible with both curated data and LLM-based reasoning

---

## Core Compatibility Signals

Pairing decisions are derived from a combination of positive and negative signals.

No single heuristic determines compatibility on its own.

---

## 1. Shared Aroma Profile

**Definition:**  
Ingredients with overlapping aroma or flavor notes tend to pair naturally.

**Examples:**
- Tomato + basil (green, herbal)
- Mushroom + thyme (earthy, woody)

**Signal Strength:** Medium?High  
**Notes:**  
Shared aroma increases harmony but may require contrast to avoid flatness.

---

## 2. Complementary Contrast

**Definition:**  
Contrasting attributes can create balance and interest when they address each other?s weaknesses.

**Common contrasts:**
- Fat ? acid
- Sweet ? bitter
- Rich ? bright
- Soft ? crisp

**Examples:**
- Rich cheese + acidic fruit
- Bitter greens + fatty dressing

**Signal Strength:** High  
**Notes:**  
Contrast is one of the strongest pairing drivers when balanced correctly.

---

## 3. Bridge Ingredients or Techniques

**Definition:**  
Two ingredients that do not pair directly may become compatible through an intermediary ingredient or shared technique.

**Examples:**
- Eggplant + miso bridged by oil and roasting
- Chocolate + chili bridged by sweetness and fat

**Signal Strength:** Medium  
**Notes:**  
Bridges often enable creative or contemporary pairings.

---

## 4. Intensity Balance

**Definition:**  
Ingredients should be matched so that neither overwhelms the other.

**Guidelines:**
- Delicate ingredients pair best with low?medium intensity partners
- Strong ingredients benefit from fat, dilution, or proportion control

**Examples:**
- Truffle with neutral carriers
- Chili moderated by dairy or starch

**Signal Strength:** High  
**Notes:**  
Poor intensity balance is a common reason for pairing failure.

---

## 5. Technique Dependency

**Definition:**  
Preparation method can significantly alter flavor, aroma, and compatibility.

**Examples:**
- Raw onion vs caramelized onion
- Fresh tomato vs roasted tomato

**Signal Strength:** Medium  
**Notes:**  
Some pairings are viable only under specific techniques.

---

## 6. Cultural and Historical Precedent

**Definition:**  
Pairings with strong traditional or regional precedent are generally reliable.

**Examples:**
- Garlic + olive oil
- Ginger + scallion

**Signal Strength:** Medium  
**Notes:**  
Precedent increases confidence but should not limit experimentation.

---

## 7. Chemical or Sensory Clash (Negative Signal)

**Definition:**  
Certain combinations create unpleasant interactions that reduce compatibility.

**Common clashes:**
- Sulfur-heavy ingredients + tannic bitterness
- Excessive bitterness without fat or sweetness
- Metallic notes with acidic environments

**Examples:**
- Overcooked brassicas with red wine
- Strong iodine notes with high tannins

**Signal Strength:** High (Negative)  
**Notes:**  
Strong clashes can override positive signals.

---

## Compatibility Outcomes

Pairings are classified into one of three outcomes:

- **Compatible** ? Strong positive signals, low risk
- **Conditional** ? Requires specific technique, proportion, or context
- **Incompatible** ? Negative signals outweigh positives

Each outcome must be accompanied by a clear explanation.

---

## Design Principles

- Compatibility is contextual, not absolute
- Explanation is as important as the verdict
- Encourage learning, not prescriptive rules

These heuristics may evolve, but changes should preserve explainability and consistency.
